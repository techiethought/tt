Hermeus Quarterhorse Reaches Mach 1.21 in Uncrewed Supersonic Test

The Quarterhorse Mk 2.1 reached a top speed of Mach 1.21 on Tuesday, May 26, during its third test flight over restricted airspace in New Mexico. The F‑16‑sized, delta‑wing aircraft used a modified Pratt & Whitney F100 afterburning turbofan to clear the sound barrier and collect transonic flight data. Hermeus says it will keep building quickly, flying Mk 2.1 further while developing Mk 2.2 with the Chimera II turbine‑based combined‑cycle engine and a planned Mk 2.3. The program blends private capital from investors including Sam Altman and Peter Thiel with Pentagon support that began with a $60 million Air Force award in 2021. Hermeus’s step‑by‑step test plan aims to lower technical risk and cost for uncrewed high‑speed aircraft, but engineers still face thermal, structural and fuel‑use challenges before sustained hypersonic flight is possible.

NASA’s MAVEN Observes Rare Solar Wind Effect Shaping Mars Atmosphere

NASA’s MAVEN mission has detected a rare solar wind phenomenon in the atmosphere of Mars, challenging long-held beliefs about the Red Planet’s magnetic environment. The discovery, published in *Nature Communications*, marks the first observation of the Zwan-Wolf effect in a planetary atmosphere. Scientists analyzed archived MAVEN data collected during a powerful solar storm that struck Mars in December 2023. The phenomenon occurs when charged particles from the Sun interact with magnetic barriers, compressing plasma into structures known as flux tubes. Researchers previously believed the effect required a strong global magnetic field, which Mars lost billions of years ago. The finding suggests similar space weather interactions may also occur on Venus and Saturn’s moon Titan, expanding scientists’ understanding of how solar wind shapes planetary atmospheres across the solar system.

Webb Separates Morning and Evening Clouds on Hot Jupiter WASP‑94A b

The Science paper published Thursday reports that JWST’s NIRISS instrument captured different transmission spectra for WASP‑94A b’s leading (morning) and trailing (evening) limbs, revealing a persistent cloud cycle. Data and 3D circulation models show mineral condensate clouds (magnesium silicates, iron and sulfides) form on the cold nightside, are carried to the morning limb by equatorial winds, and then evaporate on the hot dayside because of ~450 K temperature contrasts. When the team analyzed the clear evening limb separately they found much lower heavy‑element abundances than earlier Hubble‑era, limb‑averaged results, revising oxygen and carbon levels to about five times Jupiter’s instead of hundreds of times. The authors report similar cloud‑cycling signatures on two other hot Jupiters, WASP‑39 b and WASP‑17 b, and say larger JWST programs and updated models are now underway to test how common this biasing weather is across exoplanets. The finding shows that treating tidally locked exoplanet atmospheres as uniform can distort chemical and formation inferences, and it gives astronomers a practical method—limb‑resolved transit spectroscopy—to reduce that bias for low‑density gas giants.
